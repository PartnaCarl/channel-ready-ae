Your partner is comparing you to three competitors right now. Do you know what they're saying?

Partners are in the business of comparison. Their customers ask them to evaluate multiple vendors. Their compensation structures often incentivize them toward the vendor with the best margin, the best deal registration terms, or the easiest sales process — not necessarily the best product. Understanding how partners compare you is not optional intelligence. It's the foundation of a defensible channel motion.

The competitive conversation in channel is different from the competitive conversation in direct sales. In direct sales, you're positioning your product against a competitor's product in front of a buyer who is evaluating both. In channel, you're positioning your partner program against a competitor's partner program in front of a rep who is deciding where to spend their time and energy. The competitive intelligence you need is program-level, not just product-level.

The Core Insight

Partners will tell you what they think you want to hear about your competitive position. They won't tell you what they actually think unless you ask in a way that makes honesty safe. The TRACE posture and NRS make that conversation possible.

What partners compare — and what they actually decide on.

What Partners Say They Compare

Product features and pricing

Partners will tell you their customers choose based on product capability and price. This is partly true. It's also the safest answer to give a vendor AE when you don't want to have a difficult conversation about program quality or rep experience.

What Partners Actually Compare

Sales support and margin

In practice, partners allocate their selling time based on: which vendor's deals are easiest to close (sales support quality), which vendor pays the most (margin and SPIF), and which vendor's AE they trust and respect. Product is the threshold — program quality is the differentiator.

What Partners Never Tell You

That your competitor's AE is better

Partners will not tell you that your competitor's channel AE is more responsive, easier to work with, or better at co-sell than you are. This is the most important competitive signal you're not getting — and the NRS-based competitive conversation is how you surface it.

The Multiplier Method Competitive Conversation

Gathering Real Competitive Intelligence Using NRS Posture

"I want to ask you something directly, and I need you to be honest with me even if the answer is uncomfortable. When you're deciding which vendor to lead with on a deal, what does [Competitor X] offer you that I'm not? I'm not asking to argue with the answer — I'm asking because I want to know what I need to fix."

The competitive conversation in a live co-sell deal — how to run it without breaking your posture.

When a partner tells you that the customer is also evaluating a competitor, your Not-OK response is to immediately mount a defense of your product. The OK/OK The Multiplier Method response is to get curious before you get defensive.

When the Partner Says "The Customer Is Also Looking at Competitor X"

"That makes sense — they should evaluate their options. What's driving their interest in [Competitor X] specifically? Is it product capability, pricing, an existing relationship, or something else?" Then listen. The answer tells you exactly what the real competitive issue is — and whether it's a product issue, a program issue, or a relationship issue that you can actually address.

Competitive Intelligence as a QBR Topic

Make competitive intelligence a standing QBR agenda item: "What are you hearing from customers about [Competitor X] that we should know?" Partners who are well-activated will bring you competitive intelligence proactively if you've established that you value it and will do something with it. Partners who are under-activated will shrug — which is also useful information.

What Not to Do With Competitive Intelligence

Don't ask partners to trash competitors in customer conversations. Don't position competitive wins as evidence that competitors are bad. Don't let competitive intelligence turn you defensive in partner calls. The AE who handles competitive pressure with composure and curiosity is the AE partners trust — and that trust is a competitive advantage no product feature can replicate.

Field notes: The competitive intelligence habits that compound over time.

Building a Channel Competitive Profile

After 6–12 months of consistent competitive conversation in QBRs and partner calls, you'll have a channel-level competitive profile that most vendor AEs never build: what competitors are leading with, where their program has gaps, which of their AEs your partners trust and why, and what your partners are telling customers about you when you're not in the room. This intelligence makes every co-sell conversation sharper.

Using Competitive Intelligence to Improve Your Program

Share competitive intelligence with your channel leadership and product team. The best channel competitive insights come from partner conversations, not market research reports. If three of your partners are telling you that a competitor's deal registration is more straightforward than yours, that's a program improvement opportunity that benefits every partner in your ecosystem — and eventually shows up as market share.

The 3-Tier Talk Track — One Insight, Three Conversations

This framework is drawn from Natalie's competitive displacement work at Mimecast, Expel, and Varonis — where the same competitive insight had to land differently with partner sellers, partner executives, and end customers. The structure transfers to any competitive conversation in any vertical.

Competitive intelligence only matters if it changes how you talk. The same competitive insight needs three different versions — one for each audience you'll deliver it to.

Tier 1 — For Partner Sellers (30 seconds)

"When a customer asks about [competitor], here's the one question that changes the conversation: [question]. That question exposes [specific gap]. You don't need to know our product deeply — you just need that one question."

Tier 2 — For Partner Executives (60 seconds)

"Your team is losing [X]% of competitive deals to [competitor] because [specific reason]. We've developed a counter-play that your reps can run in 30 seconds. Here's what the data shows, and here's what we're asking from your team to test it."

Tier 3 — For Customers (30 seconds, during a partner-led conversation)

"Most organizations in your space started with [competitor approach]. What they found after 6–12 months was [specific limitation]. The question worth asking is whether that's a risk you're comfortable accepting — or whether it's worth 30 minutes to see the alternative."

Field Note

If you can't deliver all three tiers from memory, your competitive intelligence isn't field-ready. Practice until the 30-second version is automatic.

Competitive Win/Loss as a QBR Input

Every quarter, ask your top partners: which three deals did we win against our main competitor, and which three did we lose? The patterns in those six deals tell you more about your competitive position than any analyst report.

Do This Monday

In your next partner conversation this week, ask one question you have never asked before: "What are you hearing from customers about [main competitor] that I should know?" Then stop talking. Do not defend your product. Do not counter the answer. Write down exactly what they say. That intelligence is worth more than any product training session you will attend this quarter.
