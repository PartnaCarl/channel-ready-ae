Why This Module Exists

Your partner has 3–5 vendors in your category. You need to know why you win — and be able to say it in 90 seconds.

Competitive intelligence in channel isn't about badmouthing competitors. It's about understanding exactly what the three-way fight looks like in your category — and positioning your product, your program, and your working relationship in a way that wins that fight before the customer meeting starts.

The channel competitive fight is more complex than a direct fight. In direct sales, you're competing with one or two alternatives for the customer's business. In channel, you're competing on multiple dimensions simultaneously: you're competing with other vendors for the partner's attention, you're competing with direct sales for the partner's trust, and you're competing with other vendors' channel motions for the customer's consideration set — all at the same time.

Sandler's approach to competitive positioning is pain-based. You don't win by being "better" — you win by being better at solving the specific pain your target customer has. That means your competitive intelligence needs to tell you: where does your product's pain pattern diverge from your competitors'? Which customers are better served by your motion and why? What does a partner need to know to position you correctly in a competitive situation?

THE CORE INSIGHT
Competitive intelligence in channel is not about features. It's about pain fit. The partner who can say "this customer's specific problem is better solved by [your product] because [specific reason tied to their pain]" wins more deals than the partner reciting a feature comparison.

The Three-Way Fight

Understanding the competitive landscape your partner operates in every day.

⚔
Your Channel Motion
  • What pain pattern does your product solve best?
  • What ICP is your product most differentiated in?
  • What does your channel program offer that competitors don't?
  • Where does your product over-perform vs. direct alternatives?
  • What is your services attach story for partners?
⚡
Direct Sales Motion
  • When does your company go direct vs. through channel?
  • How do you prevent channel conflict with your own direct team?
  • What happens when a partner and your direct team compete?
  • What deal protection does your channel program offer?
  • Is direct a threat to your partners — and how do you address it?
🛡
Competitor Channel Motion
  • How do competitors' channel programs differ from yours?
  • Where do they win — and what is their pain fit story?
  • What do their partners say about working with them?
  • What pain are their partners experiencing with them?
  • Where are their partners under-supported or underincentivized?

Building a Pain-Based Competitive Brief

COMPETITIVE BRIEF STRUCTURE
Competitor Name: [Name]

Pain they solve best: [Specific problem where they have genuine advantage — be honest]

Pain where we win: [Specific problem where your product is more differentiated]

Their channel motion weakness: [What their partners complain about most — sourced from partner conversations, not marketing material]

Your positioning when they come up in a deal: [Two sentences. Pain-based. No badmouthing.]

One page. Updated every quarter. Every partner rep who sells your product should have this.

The Pricing Objection — Sandler's Approach

What Not to Do

Immediately justify your price. Offer a discount. Say "but we have better features." All of these confirm the partner's framing that this is a price decision. It's not — it's a pain fit decision.

What to Do (Sandler)

Get curious. "Is the other product solving the same specific problem, or are we comparing on price because the problems look similar on the surface?" Pain-fit differentiation is the only answer to a price objection that doesn't devalue your product.

Competitive Intelligence in Action

Using competitive knowledge in real partner and customer conversations.

Sourcing Intelligence from Partners

Your partners are your best competitive intelligence source. They work with multiple vendors in your category every week. They know what your competitors are doing, what their channel programs look like, and what their customers are saying. The question is whether you're asking. At every QBR and monthly sync, include a competitive intelligence question: "What are you hearing from your customers about [competitor]? What are they winning on? Where are they falling short?"

Positioning in a Three-Way Customer Meeting

COMPETITIVE POSITIONING SCRIPT
When a customer mentions they're also talking to a competitor:

"That makes sense — they're a solid option in [use case where they're strong]. What we typically find is that customers who are dealing with [your specific pain pattern] find that our approach works better because [specific reason tied to their pain — not features]. Can I ask — is [specific pain element] something you're experiencing, or is it more of a [competitor's pain pattern] situation?"

You're positioning by pain pattern, not feature comparison. The customer identifies their situation. You're helping them see which solution fits their specific problem better.

When a Partner Sells Your Competitor Instead

Sometimes a partner will take an opportunity to a competitor instead of to you. This is not betrayal — it's business. Your response should be curious, not defensive: "Help me understand why you went that direction on this one. Was it the customer's specific requirement, or was there something about our program that made it a harder choice?" The answer gives you competitive intelligence and a chance to address a gap in your program.

From the Field

What experienced channel AEs know about competitive intelligence that new ones don't.

The Best Competitive Intelligence Is Longitudinal

A single competitor conversation tells you what's happening right now. A pattern of competitor conversations over six months tells you where the market is moving. Track what you hear from partners and customers about competitors over time. Update your competitive brief quarterly. The AE who knows the competitive landscape better than anyone else in their company becomes indispensable to their partners — and to their own leadership.

When Competitors Are Better

SANDLER RULE ON HONESTY
If a competitor genuinely solves a specific customer's problem better than you do, say so. "I want to be straight with you — for this specific use case, they might actually be the better fit. Where we tend to win is [specific scenario]. Is that the situation you're in?" This counterintuitive honesty — which Sandler calls "being OK/OK" — builds more trust with partners and customers than competitive spin ever will. And it ensures that when you do say you're the better fit, they believe you.

Channel vs. Direct Conflict — The Conversation You Must Have

Every partner will eventually ask you whether your direct sales team will compete with them on their accounts. Have a clear, honest answer prepared before they ask it. If your company has channel conflict, acknowledge it and explain the deal protection mechanism. If your company has a channel-first policy, explain how it works. Vague answers to this question destroy partner trust faster than almost anything else you can do.
