Partners stall. NRS is the only tool that makes them move or admit why they won't.

Negative Reverse Selling is The Multiplier Method's counter-intuitive response to stalls, objections, and ambiguity. Instead of pushing harder when a partner goes quiet or asks for more resources without commitment, you pull back. You create space. You suggest that maybe this isn't the right fit — and you mean it. The NRS only works when you're genuinely willing to walk away.

In channel sales, stalling is a structural problem. Partners have too many vendors competing for their time and attention. Saying yes costs them nothing. Doing nothing costs them nothing. The only way to create urgency in a relationship where the partner bears no short-term cost of inaction is to introduce the possibility that your offer might disappear — and mean it when you do.

The Core Insight

The partner who asks for more resources without committing to a specific deal is not a serious partner. NRS surfaces that truth in a way that either accelerates the relationship or ends it cleanly — both outcomes are better than the indefinite stall.

What You'll Learn

The mechanics of NRS and why it works psychologically. The three most common partner stall patterns and the NRS response for each. How to distinguish genuine hesitation from delay tactics.

What You'll Practice

Delivering NRS in a partner conversation without sounding passive-aggressive or manufactured. Running the "maybe we're not the right fit" conversation through to a clear outcome. Using NRS in email when the partner goes dark.

What Changes After

Your pipeline gets cleaner. Partners who aren't serious exit faster. Partners who are serious accelerate. Your forecasting accuracy improves because every partner in your active pipeline has survived a NRS challenge.

The three partner stall patterns — and the NRS response for each.

Stall Pattern 1 — The Perpetual "We're Interested"

The partner attends every call, responds to every email positively, expresses enthusiasm for the product — and registers zero deals in four months. They're interested. They're just not doing anything.

NRS Response

"I've appreciated the conversations we've had, but I'm not seeing the deal activity that would tell me this partnership is working. I'm wondering if the timing is just off right now — or if there's something about the program that isn't working for you. Either way, I'd rather know. Should we put this on pause and revisit in a quarter when you have something specific to work on together?"

Stall Pattern 2 — The Resource Request Without Commitment

The partner asks for a demo, a custom proposal, an architecture review, a customer case study — and gives nothing in return. No deal name, no customer, no commitment to next steps. They want your IP without skin in the game.

NRS Response

"I want to make sure I'm investing our resources where they'll actually move a deal forward. Before I put together the architecture review, can you tell me specifically which customer this is for and what their decision timeline looks like? If there's no specific deal attached, I'm not sure this is the best use of our time together right now."

Stall Pattern 3 — The Go-Dark After a Strong Meeting

You had a great call. Strong energy, mutual excitement, clear next steps agreed. Then nothing. Two weeks of silence despite follow-up emails.

NRS Response — The One Email That Works

Subject: "Okay to close this out?" Body: "I haven't heard back, and I don't want to keep following up if the timing has shifted. If this isn't the right time for us to work together, just say so — I'll close the file and we can revisit down the road. If you do want to move forward, let me know and we'll pick up where we left off."

NRS in practice — what it feels like to run it live.

The most common mistake with NRS is delivering it as a script instead of a genuine position. Partners can hear the difference between "I'm reading a technique" and "I actually mean what I'm saying." NRS only creates urgency when the AE is genuinely willing to accept the outcome that this isn't the right fit.

The Internal Check Before Running NRS

Before You Run NRS, Ask Yourself This

Am I genuinely willing to walk away from this partner if they confirm this isn't the right time? If the answer is no — if you'll follow up anyway regardless of their response — don't run NRS. You'll get a one-time acceleration and then the partner will learn that your "maybe this isn't right" means nothing. NRS only works as a repeatable tool when your follow-through is consistent with what you say.

NRS in a Live Conversation

Mid-Conversation NRS — Partner Asking for More Demos Without Committing

"You know, I'm starting to wonder if our product is actually the right fit for your customers right now. I've been hearing a lot of interest but I'm not seeing the deal registrations that would tell me there's a real opportunity here. Maybe we should step back and figure out whether we're the right vendor for you to be leading with — or whether there's a different way to work together."

Field notes: What NRS reveals about your partner portfolio.

NRS as a Portfolio Diagnostic

Run NRS on every partner in your portfolio who has been "active" for 90+ days without registering a qualified deal. The results will tell you the truth about your pipeline faster than any other diagnostic. Some partners will re-engage with urgency — those are the real ones. Some will confirm that the timing isn't right. A few will disappear entirely. All three outcomes improve your portfolio quality.

What Happens After a Successful NRS

When NRS surfaces real engagement — when the partner responds with a specific customer, a deal registration, or a concrete next step — something important has happened: the partner has now demonstrated commitment. They've earned the co-sell investment that you were being asked to make for free. That's the shift NRS is designed to create.
