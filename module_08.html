Why This Module Exists

Channel deals don't stall randomly. They stall because the deal missed a Sandler checkpoint.

Every partner deal that is stuck somewhere in pipeline missed a qualification gate at an earlier stage. This module maps those gates so you can diagnose exactly where a deal broke down — and prevent it from happening in the first place.

Most channel deal pipelines are filled with deals at "proposal sent" or "customer evaluation" that have been there for 60, 90, 120 days. The AE keeps calling it pipeline because removing it feels like failure. The partner keeps calling it live because they don't want to tell you it's dead.

The Sandler stage-gate model applies a checkpoint to every deal stage: before you advance a deal from one stage to the next, you must be able to confirm specific criteria — pain identified, decision-maker confirmed, timeline committed, budget acknowledged, UFC in place. If you can't confirm the criteria, the deal doesn't advance. It either goes back or comes out.

THE CORE INSIGHT
A deal that doesn't meet the criteria to advance is not a stalled deal — it's an unqualified deal. The distinction matters because it tells you exactly what to do next: either get the missing information, or remove the deal from active pipeline.

The Six-Stage Deal Map

From first contact to closed PO — with Sandler checkpoints at each gate.

S1SOURCE

Stage 1 — Partner Sourcing

Partner identifies a potential opportunity. Checkpoint: Partner can name the account, the contact, and the presenting problem. Vague "we have a few accounts that might need this" does not pass Stage 1. Name and company required to advance.

S2QUALIFY

Stage 2 — Partner + AE Qualification

Joint qualification call or meeting. Checkpoint: Pain confirmed at Level 2+ (Pain Funnel), economic buyer identified, preliminary timeline provided. UFC in place for next step. Deal registration submitted.

S3DISCOVER

Stage 3 — Discovery with End Customer

Joint customer call. Checkpoint: Pain confirmed at Level 3+ (emotional impact articulated), budget range acknowledged, decision process mapped, decision-maker confirmed to be in the room before proposal. UFC agreed for next step.

S4PROPOSE

Stage 4 — Proposal and Presentation

Formal proposal delivered. Checkpoint: Economic buyer present for proposal review (not just the partner), decision timeline reconfirmed, customer can articulate why they need this now. No proposal to an empty room or through a third party without economic buyer presence.

S5COMMIT

Stage 5 — Decision and Commitment

Customer commits verbally. Checkpoint: Verbal yes from economic buyer, PO process initiated, legal/procurement timeline established, any remaining objections surfaced and addressed. UFC for PO issuance date.

S6CLOSE

Stage 6 — Closed / PO Received

Purchase order received. The only finish line. A verbal yes without a PO date is still Stage 5. Implementation kickoff scheduled. Partner margin confirmed. Win Wire submitted to channel team.

Running Deals Through the Model

Applying the stage-gate system to your real pipeline.

The first time you run your current pipeline against the stage-gate criteria, you'll likely find that 30-50% of your deals don't meet the criteria for the stage they're assigned to. This is not a pipeline crisis — it's an accuracy improvement. Better to know now than at the end of the quarter.

The Stage Gate Diagnostic Conversation

WITH YOUR PARTNER
"I want to do a quick review of [account name]. When you talked to them last, did you confirm who has budget authority? Has the customer said they need to make a decision by a specific date? I'm trying to figure out where this actually is so we can plan the right next step."

If the partner can't answer both questions, the deal is at Stage 1, not Stage 3 where it's been sitting for six weeks.

When to Remove a Deal from Pipeline

Keep in Pipeline

Partner can answer Stage criteria questions. Decision-maker is engaged. Timeline exists. There is a confirmed next step with a date. Pain is at Level 3+.

Remove from Pipeline

No response from partner for 2+ weeks with no explanation. Cannot name decision-maker after Stage 3. Timeline keeps shifting with no new information. Customer said they were interested months ago and has gone silent.

Removing Deals Without Damaging the Partner Relationship

Removing a deal from pipeline is not a judgment on the partner. It's an accuracy improvement. Frame it that way.
REMOVAL CONVERSATION
"I want to take [account] off my active forecast for this quarter — not because I think the deal is dead, but because I don't have enough confirmation that the timeline is real. I'd rather track it as a follow-up and bring it back when we have the decision-maker confirmed. Does that work for you?"
From the Field

What the stage-gate model reveals about a partner's deal management skills.

The Partner Readiness Signal

How quickly a partner can answer your Stage 2 and Stage 3 checkpoint questions tells you everything about their sales discipline. A partner who can immediately tell you the decision-maker's name, their timeline, and their pain is a partner who qualifies their own opportunities. A partner who responds to every checkpoint question with "let me follow up" is a partner whose deals will stall at every stage.

Using the Stage Map in Your QBR

The Sandler stage-gate model becomes your QBR pipeline review tool. For each deal in pipeline, ask: What stage is it in? What is the checkpoint criteria? What is missing? What is the next step? This structure transforms the QBR from a slide-deck review into a working session that produces real decisions. (Module 09 covers this in full.)

FIELD RULE
A deal that has been at the same stage for more than 30 days without movement is a stalled deal. Run the diagnostic, apply NRS if appropriate (Module 06), and make a decision: advance it or remove it. Never let a deal sit in limbo indefinitely. Limbo is not a pipeline stage.
