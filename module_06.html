Channel deals die in predictable places. The Multiplier Method checkpoints prevent each one.

A direct-sales deal is a conversation between two parties. A channel deal is a conversation between three parties minimum — vendor AE, partner rep, and end customer — and often four or five when you add partner leadership, technical resources, and distributor support. Each additional party is an additional point where the deal can stall, misalign, or die without anyone understanding why.

The Anatomy of a Partner Deal maps the channel deal lifecycle into six stages, each with a Multiplier Method checkpoint that must be cleared before progressing. The checkpoint isn't bureaucracy — it's a diagnostic test. When a deal passes the checkpoint at each stage, both the vendor and the partner have confirmed that the deal is real, qualified, and progressing for the right reasons. When a deal fails a checkpoint, you find out before you've invested the resources that would have been wasted on a deal that was never going to close.

The Core Insight

Most channel deals don't die at the end — they die because something that should have been qualified at Stage 2 was allowed to progress to Stage 5 unchecked. The anatomy framework surfaces those failures early.

Six stages. Six The Multiplier Method checkpoints. One clean pipeline.

Stage 1 — Partner Interest

Checkpoint: Partner Fit Filter + qualification

Has the partner identified a specific customer with real pain that your product solves? Is there economic motivation for the partner to sell this deal? Can they name the customer and the pain in the same sentence?

Stage 2 — Deal Registration

Checkpoint: CAA with partner

Has the partner registered the deal with a named customer and a defined scope? Is there a CAA in place — agreed agenda for the co-sell engagement, defined roles for vendor AE and partner rep, and a named next step with a date?

Stage 3 — Customer Discovery

Checkpoint: Partner Fit Filter complete

Has real customer pain been diagnosed at all three levels — surface problem, business impact, and real pain (the cost of inaction)? Has the customer confirmed the pain is a priority, not just an interest?

Stage 4 — Solution and Proposal

Checkpoint: Budget and decision process confirmed

Does the customer have budget allocated or accessible? Has the decision process been mapped — who approves, what the timeline is, and what the evaluation criteria are? Is there a specific decision date on the calendar?

Stage 5 — Evaluation

Checkpoint: No-Free-Consulting enforced

Is any POC, architecture work, or custom solution being done under a signed evaluation agreement with defined success criteria? Is the partner's commitment to close contingent on evaluation results — not contingent on "how the customer feels"?

Stage 6 — Close

Checkpoint: CAA with partner + customer confirmation

Has the partner confirmed the customer is ready to order? Is there a specific date and method for closing — PO, order form, channel submission? Does the partner have everything they need, or are there open items that will delay the close?

Where channel deals actually die — and the diagnostic for each.

Most channel deals don't fail at Stage 6. They fail because a Stage 2 or Stage 3 gap was allowed to persist into Stage 5 — when everyone is invested in the deal and no one wants to acknowledge that it was never fully qualified.

The Most Common Death Points

Between Stage 2 and 3

Deal registered, discovery never happens

The partner registers the deal to protect it, then waits for the vendor AE to do all the customer work. Partner Fit Filter checkpoint was never cleared. The deal sits in the pipeline for 90 days as "in discussion."

Between Stage 4 and 5

Evaluation starts without budget confirmation

The partner pushes into evaluation before confirming that the customer has budget authority and a real purchase timeline. The POC completes successfully, the customer loves the product — and then procurement says there's no budget until next fiscal year.

The Weekly Deal Review Framework

Deal Review Questions — Use in Your Pipeline Meeting Starting This Week

For every deal: Which stage is this at? What is The Multiplier Method checkpoint for this stage, and has it been cleared? What is the specific next action, who owns it, and what is the date? If we don't get that next action by that date, what happens to the deal?

Field notes: What a clean pipeline actually looks like.

The Pipeline Truthfulness Test

Apply the Anatomy framework to every deal in your current partner pipeline. For each deal, ask: at what stage is this deal genuinely at — not where the partner says it is, but where The Multiplier Method checkpoints confirm it is? Most AEs find that 40–60% of their "active" pipeline is actually Stage 1 or Stage 2 masquerading as Stage 4 or Stage 5. That's the pipeline problem, and the Anatomy framework surfaces it in a single pipeline review.

Using the Anatomy Framework With Partners

Share the framework with your partners. Make it the basis for deal review conversations: "Let's walk through the Anatomy on this deal together — I want to make sure we're both clear on where we are and what the next checkpoint is." Partners who engage with this process become better at managing their own pipeline. Partners who resist it are usually managing deals that haven't cleared Stage 2.

Four Co-Selling Motions — Match the Motion to the Partner Type

These motions are drawn from Natalie's experience building channel programs at Expel, Mimecast, Okta, and Varonis — adapted here as transferable frameworks for any B2B technology vertical. The partner types and motion logic apply whether you're selling security, infrastructure, retail, or SaaS.

The type of partner determines the co-sell motion — and which stages of the deal lifecycle you'll spend the most time on. Running the wrong motion with the wrong partner type is why deals stall.

Motion A — Big Box Partners (CDW, SHI, Zones)

Find 3–5 champion regions. Within each region, identify the security-aware sellers who are already having the right conversations. Enable them directly — skip the partner's national program office until you have proof points.

Motion B — Security Advisory / Services-Led (Guidepoint, Optiv, Presidio)

Map to their practice areas. Co-develop a joint assessment play. Enable consultants who are already in customer environments — they're your force multiplier.

Motion C — M365 Attach / Licensing (SoftwareOne, Insight)

Simple qualification: "Are you relying solely on native Microsoft security for email, identity, or endpoint?" That's the opening. Position as a natural extension of their existing licensing motion.

Motion D — Regional / Emerging

Bring deals first. Get on one team call (15 minutes) after your first meeting. Assess their technical capability and sales maturity before investing in formal enablement.
