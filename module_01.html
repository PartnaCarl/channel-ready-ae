Why This Module Exists

Partners are not customers. Stop treating them like one.

If you walk into your first partner call sounding like a direct sales pitch, you won't get a second call. This module prevents that.

Most AEs treat partners like customers — pitching product, pushing urgency, closing. Partners are independent businesses with their own P&L, their own priorities, and their own judgment about which vendors are worth their time. This module teaches you how they think, how they make money, and what that means for how you talk to them.

You're about to learn the economics that explain every partner behavior you'll encounter: why some reps push your product and others ignore it, why deal registration matters more than your pitch deck, and why the partner's services business is more important to them than your product will ever be.

THE UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH
Your partner's rep does not care about your product. They care about making money, serving their customers, and not wasting time. Your job is to make selling your product the easiest, most profitable thing they do this week. If you can't do that, they'll sell the vendor who can.

What You'll Learn

How VARs generate revenue — margin stacks, deal registration economics, services attach rates, and MDF. The financial model that explains every partner decision.

What You'll Practice

Mapping a partner's revenue model for your specific product. Identifying your services pull-through story. Writing a partner-first introduction email.

What Changes After

You stop pitching and start enabling. You lead with the partner's economics, not your features. Your first partner meeting goes from awkward to productive.

The Money

Four ways a VAR rep earns on a deal.

Understanding these four revenue streams is the foundation of every conversation you'll have with a partner. When you know how they make money, you know how to position your product as a revenue opportunity — not a favor to you.

1. Product Margin (8–15% hardware, 15–30% software)

The base margin on selling your product. This is the least exciting revenue for a VAR rep — it's thin, competitive, and shrinking in most categories. Hardware margins have compressed for a decade. Software margins are better but require the rep to understand your licensing model. If your product margin is your only value proposition to the partner, you're losing to the vendor with better services attach.

2. Deal Registration Uplift (+5–15 points)

When a partner registers a deal with you, they get additional margin protection — typically 5–15 percentage points above standard discount. This is the single most powerful financial incentive you control. A rep who registers a $100K deal at 25% margin (with deal reg) vs. 12% margin (without) is making $13,000 more on the same sale. That's why deal reg matters — and why you need to make the process fast and easy.

3. Services Attach (30–50% margin)

This is where the real money is. A CDW rep selling a $50K hardware deal with $30K in attached services earns more on the services than the hardware. Services include: deployment, imaging, staging, managed services, monitoring, custom development, security architecture, and ongoing support. Your job as a vendor: make the services story obvious. Map your product to the partner's services portfolio before your first meeting.

4. SPIFs & Incentives (Variable)

Vendor-funded bonuses for specific actions: $25 for attending training, $100 for scheduling a demo, $200 for a customer meeting, $300–$500 for a deal registration. These are cash payments on top of margin. They're small individually but they determine which vendor a rep thinks about first when a customer asks "what do you recommend?" SPIFs are the attention economy of channel sales.

The Math That Matters

EXAMPLE
A $75K deal through a VAR with full services and deal reg:

Product margin (15% on $75K): $11,250
Deal reg uplift (additional 10%): $7,500
Services attach ($40K at 40% margin): $16,000
SPIF payment: $500

Total partner rep earnings: $35,250 — and $16,000 of it came from services. That's why the partner's services story is more important to them than your product features.

How You Talk

Stop selling to your partner. Start enabling them to sell for you.

CORE INSIGHT
Direct sales language: "I have a great opportunity for you."
Partner language: "I have a customer in your territory who needs help — here's how we can work this together."

The shift is from pitching to serving. Partners respond to vendors who make their job easier, not vendors who add to their workload.

What to Say vs. What Not to Say

❌ Don't Say This

"Let me tell you about our amazing product."
"We need to get some pipeline going."
"Can you introduce me to your customers?"
"We're the best in our category."
"I want to schedule a deep-dive for your whole team."
"Any pipeline updates?"

✓ Say This Instead

"I've got 3 accounts in your territory — can we look at them together?"
"Here's how my product maps to the services your team already sells."
"I'll submit the deal reg in your name — you get full margin protection."
"What's your team's biggest focus this quarter? I want to see if we fit."
"I'll keep this to 30 minutes and give your reps a reason to call me."
"Here's what happened since last week — [specific account updates]."

The Three Rules of Partner Communication

Rule 1 — Lead with Their Economics

Every conversation starts with what the partner gains — margin, services attach, SPIF payments. Your product features are supporting evidence, not the headline.

Rule 2 — Bring Accounts, Not Pitches

Never show up to a partner meeting without at least 3 named accounts to discuss. Accounts are currency. Pitches are noise. A partner rep who sees you bringing pipeline will take your calls.

Rule 3 — Be the Easiest Vendor

Fast email responses. Clean deal reg submissions. Accurate quotes. Simple battle cards. The partner rep chooses the vendor who makes their day easier — not the vendor with the best technology.

Preparation

Before your first partner meeting — the complete checklist.

Do not attend your first partner meeting without completing these items. An unprepared first meeting is worse than no meeting — it brands you as another time-wasting vendor in a rep's crowded inbox.
Research the Partner
Identify the partner's tier status with at least 3 competing vendors
Tells you where you rank in their priority stack before you walk in.
Map the partner org chart: VP, DM, SM, and your assigned AM contacts — names, titles, LinkedIn
You need names at every level. Start with the AM, but know who's above them.
Understand the partner's services portfolio — what do they sell alongside hardware/software?
This is the key to everything. Their services attach is your lever for relevance.
Know the partner's current quarterly focus (earnings calls, press releases, partner portal)
The Cord Principle (Module 03) starts here — map your product to their focus.
Review the partner's recent wins and press coverage — what are they bragging about?
Reference their wins in conversation. It shows you did your homework and respect their business.
Prepare Your Materials
Build a 1-page battle card with qualification signals (not a product brochure)
"SELL THIS IF: [3 customer signals]." One page. That's all a rep will read.
Map your product to the partner's services portfolio — where's the pull-through?
Write one sentence: "When you sell [our product], you can attach [these services] worth $[X]."
Pull 3–5 accounts in the partner's territory from your pipeline or ICP
Never show up empty-handed. Accounts are currency. Bring them to every meeting.
Prepare your deal registration process overview (how fast, how simple, what margin)
The partner's first question will be "what's the deal reg process?" Have the answer ready.
Set the Meeting Up for Success
Schedule 30 minutes max — not 60. Respect their time.
A 60-minute first meeting signals you don't understand how busy partner reps are.
Write an agenda in 3 bullets: (1) intro, (2) 3 accounts to discuss, (3) how deal reg / SPIFs work
Send the agenda in advance. Let them see you're bringing value, not a product pitch.
Prepare your "ask" — what do you specifically want from this meeting?
"Can we review these 3 accounts and see if a joint call makes sense?" Not "can you sell my product?"

Communication Templates

The emails that open the door.

These templates are structured for a first-time vendor-partner interaction. Adapt the specifics — the structure and tone are what matter.

Template 1 — First Introduction to a New Partner AM

To: [Partner AM Name]
Subject: [Your Company] — intro + a few accounts in your territory
Hi [Name], I'm [Your Name] at [Company] — I'll be your vendor contact going forward. Quick intro and a reason to connect: I've pulled 3 accounts in your territory that are either in my pipeline or match our ideal profile. I'd love 20 minutes to walk through them together. The accounts: → [Account 1] — [1-sentence context] → [Account 2] — [1-sentence context] → [Account 3] — [1-sentence context] I'm not here to give you a product pitch — I'm here to help you close business.
