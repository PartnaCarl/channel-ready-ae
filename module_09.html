Why This Module Exists

Most QBRs are expensive slide presentations that produce no accountability.

The average channel QBR follows the same pattern: AE presents numbers, partner nods, both parties agree things will be better next quarter, no specific commitments are made, nothing changes. The Sandler QBR breaks this pattern at every step.

The problem with the traditional QBR format is structural: it's designed for presentation, not for accountability. The AE presents their slides. The partner presents theirs. Everyone is polite. Difficult conversations are avoided. No one commits to anything specific enough to be measured. Three months later, the same conversation happens again.

A Sandler-based QBR operates differently from the first minute. It opens with a UFC that establishes what success looks like by the end of the meeting. It reviews last quarter's results honestly — including where things fell short and why. It produces specific, named, dated commitments from both parties. And it ends with a UFC on next steps. This is the QBR that partners remember and prepare for — because they know you will hold them to what they say.

THE CORE INSIGHT
A QBR that doesn't produce named commitments is a status update with overhead. If you leave without knowing exactly who is doing what by when, you didn't run a QBR — you ran a meeting.

What You'll Learn

The Sandler QBR structure from UFC open through commitment close. How to have the honest conversation about why last quarter missed. How to get real commitments without threatening the relationship.

What You'll Practice

Opening a QBR with a UFC. Running an honest last-quarter debrief. Driving to specific, named action items with dates and owners. Closing the QBR with a confirmed next step.

What Changes After

Your QBRs become the partner meeting they actually prepare for. Partner accountability increases because commitments are explicit. Your forecast accuracy improves because you're hearing honest assessments.

The Sandler QBR Structure

A 90-minute QBR that produces accountability instead of theater.

Section 1 — UFC Open (10 min)

QBR UFC SCRIPT
"Before we get into the data, I want to set up what I'm hoping we accomplish today. I want to look at last quarter honestly — not defensively, just accurately — and I want us to leave with a specific plan for next quarter that we're both committed to. That means I'm going to say some things that might be uncomfortable, and I'm asking you to do the same. By the end, I want a list of named action items with dates and owners. Is that the kind of QBR you're prepared for today?"

Section 2 — Last Quarter Debrief (30 min)

Review the numbers without spin. If you missed, say you missed. Then ask why — and let the partner answer. The Sandler approach is to present the data and ask the partner to diagnose before you offer your own assessment. "Here's what we agreed on last quarter. Here's what happened. What's your read on the gap?"

Review — What Was Committed

Pull up the commitment list from the last QBR. Read each item aloud. Was it completed, partially completed, or missed? No editorializing — just the facts. Let the partner respond to each item.

Diagnose — What Caused the Gap

For each miss, ask: "What happened?" Not "whose fault was it?" but "what was the root cause?" You're building a shared understanding of the friction, not assigning blame. That shared understanding becomes the basis for next quarter's plan.

Section 3 — Next Quarter Planning (30 min)

Based on the debrief diagnosis, build a plan that addresses the actual root causes. Don't just increase the target — change the approach. Each action item must have a name, a date, and a success criterion. "We'll try to do more joint calls" is not an action item. "We'll schedule two joint customer calls per week starting the first week of the quarter, with [rep name] as the owner" is an action item.

Section 4 — Commitment Close (10 min)

COMMITMENT CLOSE SCRIPT
"Before we finish, I want to read back what we've committed to and make sure we're both clear. [Read each item.] Are you comfortable committing to these specifically? I'm going to send you this list today and I'll reference it at our next QBR. And from my side, I'm committing to [your items]. Does that work?"
QBR in Action

The conversations most channel AEs avoid — and why you shouldn't.

The Partner Who Wants to Skip the Debrief

Some partners try to jump straight to "so what are we doing next quarter?" They don't want to look at last quarter's misses in detail. This is a red flag — and a reason to stay with the debrief, not skip it. You cannot plan effectively without understanding what caused last quarter's results.

RESPONSE TO "LET'S JUST FOCUS FORWARD"
"I hear you — and I want to focus forward too. But I've found that when we skip the honest look at what happened, we end up planning the same thing and getting the same result. Can we spend just 15 minutes on the diagnosis? I think we'll be able to plan much better after."

The Partner Who Has Excuses for Everything

Every miss has an explanation. Market conditions, personnel changes, customer delays. These may all be real. Your job is not to dispute them — it's to ask what you and the partner will do differently to account for these realities in the next quarter's plan. "Given that [reason], what changes do we need to make to our approach?" That question moves from excuse to accountability.

The Partner Who Won't Commit to Specifics

DRIVING TO SPECIFICS
When a partner says "we'll try to bring in more opportunities," your response is: "I appreciate that — let's make it more specific so we can both track it. If we were going to say that in a way I could measure at the next QBR, what would that look like exactly? Is it number of deals registered? Number of joint calls? A specific revenue number?" Keep asking until you have something measurable.
From the Field

What separates a QBR that matters from a QBR that gets rescheduled.

The Pre-QBR Send

Send the data package at least 48 hours before the QBR: last quarter's commitment list, pipeline summary, revenue and deal metrics, and the proposed agenda with your UFC stated in writing. Partners who show up to a Sandler QBR prepared are more willing to have honest conversations. The pre-send signals that this is a different kind of meeting.

The Post-QBR Follow-Through

Within 24 hours of the QBR, send a written summary of every commitment made — who owns it, what it is, and when it's due. Reference this document at every monthly sync between now and the next QBR. Partners who know you track commitments start taking them more seriously in the moment they make them.

THE ACCOUNTABILITY LOOP
QBR commitments reviewed at monthly syncs. Monthly syncs reference QBR commitments. Next QBR opens with the previous QBR's commitment list. This loop is what turns a once-a-quarter conversation into a continuous accountability system. Partners who experience this loop for two consecutive quarters almost always produce more than partners in a traditional QBR program.
