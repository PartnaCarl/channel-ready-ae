Why This Module Exists

Most channel AEs never disqualify a partner. That's why their territory is full of noise.

A partner who is enthusiastic but misaligned with your product is worse than no partner at all. They consume your time, expect support, and produce nothing. Qualification cuts through this fast.

The channel orthodoxy says more partners means more coverage means more revenue. The reality is the opposite. Every partner you onboard improperly is a commitment you've made — to training, to enablement, to QBRs, to co-sell calls. Bad-fit partners crowd out the partners who could actually produce.

Sandler's qualification approach starts with pain. If a partner doesn't feel real pain with their current vendor situation — if switching to your product doesn't solve a genuine problem in their business — they will not prioritize you. No amount of SPIF money or deal reg margin changes that. This module teaches you to find the pain, evaluate the profile, and exit fast when neither exists.

THE CORE INSIGHT
Qualification is a gift you give both parties. The partner who isn't a fit doesn't want to waste their time on your product either. Fast, clear disqualification leaves both of you free to find relationships that actually work.

The Qualification System

Must-have profile + Pain Funnel = fast, defensible qualification decisions.

Step 1 — Build Your Must-Have Partner Profile

Before you can qualify a partner, you need a clear profile of what a good partner looks like. This is not aspirational — it is a minimum bar. Partners who don't meet must-have criteria should be disqualified regardless of their enthusiasm.

✓
Must-Have Criteria
  • Sells into your target verticals or segments Non-Negotiable
  • At least one rep with relevant technical knowledge Non-Negotiable
  • Three or more active accounts in your product sweet spot Non-Negotiable
  • Leadership willing to participate in a QBR within 90 days Non-Negotiable
  • Can commit a named rep to co-sell engagements Non-Negotiable
○
Nice-to-Have Criteria
  • Existing relationship with a complementary vendor Helpful
  • Dedicated pre-sales engineering team Helpful
  • MDF-eligible and willing to invest in demand gen Helpful
  • Previous experience with your product category Helpful
  • Geographic coverage in a strategic market for you Helpful

Step 2 — Run the Pain Funnel

The Pain Funnel is a Sandler technique for moving from surface-level interest to genuine business pain. In a partner context, you're probing the partner's own pain with their current situation — not their end-customer's pain. That comes later, in the deal cycle.

PARTNER PAIN FUNNEL QUESTIONS
Level 1 — Surface: "Tell me about your current vendor lineup in this category."

Level 2 — Specifics: "Where are the gaps? What do your reps say most often when customers ask about this space?"

Level 3 — Emotional Impact: "What does it cost you when you can't satisfy a customer in this category? Does it affect other deals?"

Level 4 — Cost of Status Quo: "If nothing changes — you keep working with your current vendor — what does that look like in 12 months?"

Step 3 — Score and Decide

Qualify — All of These True

Partner meets all must-have criteria. They articulate real pain at Level 3 or deeper in the funnel. They can name specific accounts and a timeline. Leadership is accessible and interested.

Disqualify — Any of These True

Missing a must-have criterion. Pain is vague or hypothetical. No named rep available. Leadership has no interest in a QBR. Partner asks for concessions before committing to anything.

Qualification in Action

How a partner qualification call actually runs.

Most channel AEs treat a partner onboarding call as a product pitch. That's backwards. The goal of the first call is to determine whether a deeper relationship is worth your time — not to convince the partner to sign up. If you're doing most of the talking, you're pitching, not qualifying.

Opening — Set the Purpose

This is a form of Up-Front Contract (covered in Module 03). Before you go anywhere, establish what the call is for and what both parties need to walk away with to make it worthwhile.
OPENING SCRIPT
"I want to be upfront about what I'm hoping to accomplish today. I'm not here to sign you up — I want to figure out whether there's a real fit between what we're doing and what you're trying to accomplish. I'll ask you some questions about your current business, and I'd ask you to do the same for me. If it makes sense to go further, we'll know by the end of this call. Does that work?"

The Middle — Pain Funnel Sequence

Run the four-level Pain Funnel questions in order. Don't rush to Level 4. Partners who feel pushed will give surface answers. Let silence work. Ask one question, then wait. The discomfort of silence often produces the most honest answers you'll get in the entire relationship.

Closing — Binary Decision

CLOSING SCRIPT
"Based on what we've discussed, I think there's a real opportunity here / I'm not sure this is the right time for us to work together. Here's my honest read: [your assessment]. What's yours?" Make the partner co-own the decision. If they're not sure either, that's a disqualify.

The Fast Exit When It Doesn't Fit

A clean no is one of the most professional things you can deliver. Partners respect AEs who don't waste time. "Based on where you are today, I don't think we're the right fit right now. Let me know if that changes — I'd rather come back when the timing is right than force something that isn't." Then stop following up.

From the Field

The patterns that separate productive partner portfolios from bloated ones.

The Most Common Qualification Mistake

Allowing enthusiasm to substitute for pain. A partner who says "we love this product, we have tons of customers who need this" is not qualified — they're excited. Excitement without named accounts, named pain, and a committed rep is not a qualified partner. Push through the enthusiasm to the specifics. Enthusiasm is not pipeline.

How to Handle the Pushy Partner Who Wants In Without Qualifying

FIELD SCENARIO
A partner contacts you wanting to sign the agreement today. They attended your webinar, they like the product, they want in. They haven't answered a single qualification question. The Sandler response: "I'm glad you're interested. Before we sign anything, I want to make sure we can actually deliver value to each other — I've seen too many partners sign up and then not produce because the fit wasn't right. Can we spend 30 minutes on the phone first?" Partners who refuse this step are not ready to be partners.

Revisiting Disqualified Partners

A disqualification is not permanent. Keep a short list of partners who showed genuine interest but weren't ready. Reach out every six months with a single question: "Has anything changed on your end that might make this a better time to talk?" If yes, re-run the qualification process from scratch. Don't skip steps because you've talked to them before.
