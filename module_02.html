Most channel AEs qualify partners the way bad salespeople qualify deals — they don't.

They take every partner call. They onboard every company that fills out the partner application. They invest business planning, co-sell support, and marketing resources into relationships that were never going to produce revenue. The Partner Fit Filter fixes this.

The Partner Fit Filter was built to uncover whether a prospect has real pain before you invest time selling to them. Applied to partner qualification, it answers the question your partner application form never asks: does this partner have a real problem that your product solves, real customers who need it, and real motivation to sell it? If the answer to any of those is no, you're looking at a partner who will consume your time and produce nothing.

The Core Insight

Qualifying partners the same way you qualify customers is not optional — it's the only way to build a channel that produces revenue. Every hour you spend on an unqualified partner is an hour you didn't spend on one who would close.

What You'll Learn

The Partner Fit Filter applied to partner qualification. Must-have vs. nice-to-have partner criteria. What to listen for in the first partner conversation that tells you whether this relationship has a future.

What You'll Practice

Running the partner onboarding conversation as a Multiplier Method qualifying call. Asking Partner Fit Filter questions without sounding like you're interrogating a prospect. Getting to a disqualification decision without burning the relationship.

What Changes After

You spend 80% of your partner time on the 20% of partners who produce revenue. Your partner pipeline gets smaller and better. Your forecast accuracy improves because every partner in it has been qualified.

The Partner Fit Filter applied to partner qualification.

The Multiplier Method's Partner Fit Filter moves from surface-level problem to real business pain through a structured sequence of questions. In a direct-sales context, you're diagnosing a buyer's pain. In a channel context, you're diagnosing the partner's pain around their existing vendor relationships and their customers' pain that your product solves.

Must-Have Partner Criteria — Qualify On These First

Customer Fit

Do they have the right customers?

Does the partner's existing customer base match the profile of companies that buy your product? If you sell mid-market cybersecurity and the partner only has SMB healthcare clients, the customer fit is poor regardless of their enthusiasm.

Motivation to Sell

Why would they choose your product over alternatives?

Is your product meaningfully better for their customers than what they already sell? Do they have economic motivation — is there margin, MDF, or compensation attached? Enthusiasm without economics doesn't close deals.

Capacity to Execute

Can they actually sell and support it?

Do they have trained reps? Do they have a pre-sales or technical resource? A partner who can sell but not support will create customer escalations that damage your brand alongside theirs.

Commitment Level

Are they willing to invest before you do?

Will they attend enablement? Will they register a deal before you do co-sell work? Will they name specific customers they're targeting? A partner who won't commit before the sale won't commit after it either.

The Disqualification Framework

Disqualification is not rejection — it's clarity. A partner who doesn't meet must-have criteria is not a bad company. They're just not the right partner for your product right now. The clean exit preserves the relationship for a future where the fit might improve.

Clean Disqualification Script

"Based on what we've talked about, I don't think the timing is right for us to invest in a formal partnership right now. Your customers are [profile] and our best-fit customers are [profile] — and I don't think we'd be setting each other up for success. I'd rather tell you that now than waste both of our time onboarding and then watching nothing close. Can we revisit this in six months if your customer base shifts?"

Running the first partner call as a qualifying conversation.

The standard first partner call runs like this: the vendor AE presents the product, the partner asks a few questions, both parties agree it sounds promising, and the partner is onboarded. Nothing is qualified. Nothing is committed. The relationship starts on a foundation of mutual enthusiasm instead of mutual understanding.

The Multiplier Method partner qualification call runs differently. The AE leads with questions, not with a pitch. The goal is diagnosis, not persuasion. By the end of the call, both parties know whether there is a real business reason to move forward — or not.

Partner Fit Filter Questions for Partner Qualification

Level 1 — Surface Problem

"Tell me about the vendors you're currently selling in this space. How's that going?" (What's the stated problem with their current vendor mix?)

Level 2 — Impact

"What does that cost you in terms of deals lost, customer escalations, or margin pressure?" (What's the business impact of the problem?)

Level 3 — Real Pain

"How long has this been an issue? Have you tried to solve it before?" (What's the depth and history of the pain? Is it real or theoretical?)

Commitment Test

"If we moved forward, what would you be willing to commit to in the first 90 days — in terms of rep enablement time, deal registration, and joint prospecting?" (Can they make a specific commitment before any investment is made?)

What the best channel AEs know about qualification that others miss.

Enthusiasm Is Not Qualification

The most dangerous partner is the one who is genuinely excited about your product. They attend every webinar, they introduce you to their leadership, they talk about all the customers they're going to bring you. And then they register zero deals in six months. Enthusiasm without customer fit and economic motivation is a distraction dressed up as opportunity.

The Pattern to Watch For

Partners who are very enthusiastic in onboarding but non-committal when you ask for specific customer names or deal registrations. This pattern almost always means the partner sees value in the relationship for reasons other than selling your product — referral fees, industry access, or just vendor relationship building. Qualify for actual deal production, not enthusiasm level.

Qualifying Is an Ongoing Process

Partner qualification doesn't end at onboarding. It's an ongoing assessment that runs through every QBR, every co-sell engagement, and every deal registration review. A partner who was qualified 18 months ago may no longer be qualified today if their business has changed, their team has turned over, or their customer base has shifted. The Partner Fit Filter questions apply at re-qualification just as they do at initial qualification.

Pull up your active partner list. Apply the four Must-Have criteria to every partner: customer fit, motivation to sell, capacity to execute, commitment level. The partners you can't afford to lose are the ones who passed all four.
