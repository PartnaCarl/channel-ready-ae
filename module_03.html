Why This Module Exists

Most channel relationships drift because no one ever says what they expect.

Ambiguity is the enemy of channel productivity. When both parties aren't clear on what they're committing to, the default outcome is inaction — and the AE always loses more than the partner.

Channel partnerships fail in slow motion. The partner doesn't reject you outright — they just deprioritize. They miss meetings. They show up unprepared to QBRs. They let pipeline rot. The root cause is almost always the same: neither party was ever explicit about what they expected from each other. The relationship is built on vague goodwill instead of clear agreements.

Sandler's Up-Front Contract (UFC) is the antidote. Before any meeting, call, engagement, or deal cycle starts, you establish — out loud, with the partner's explicit agreement — what you're both there to accomplish, what you each need from the other, and what a successful outcome looks like. This changes the dynamic from passive goodwill to active, mutual accountability.

THE CORE INSIGHT
Partners respect vendors who are clear about expectations. The partner who treats you as a peer sets clear expectations of their own. When you arrive with a UFC, you signal that you operate the same way — and that signal builds more credibility than any product pitch.

What You'll Learn

The five components of a Sandler Up-Front Contract. How to adapt the UFC for partner meetings, co-sell engagements, deal cycles, and QBRs.

What You'll Practice

Opening every partner interaction with a clear UFC. Handling partner pushback on the UFC. Closing the loop at the end of every meeting with a confirmed next step.

What Changes After

You stop leaving meetings wondering what happens next. Partners show up prepared because they know what's expected. Deal cycles accelerate because ambiguity is eliminated early.

The UFC Structure

Five components. Every one of them required.

An Up-Front Contract has five elements. Skip any one of them and you have a weaker agreement — one that a partner can slide out of when it becomes inconvenient. All five must be established explicitly.

1
Purpose — Why We're Here
  • State the purpose of this specific interaction Required
  • Be direct — not "to touch base" but "to agree on which accounts we're co-selling this quarter" Required
  • Ask the partner to confirm or add to the purpose Required
2
Time — How Long We Have
  • Agree on the time available upfront, not at the end Required
  • Confirm both parties have the full time blocked Required
  • Establishes accountability if someone cuts it short Required
3
Agenda — What We'll Cover
  • State your agenda items and ask for theirs Required
  • Combine into a shared agenda before you start Required
  • Creates shared ownership of the conversation Required
4
Outcome — What a Win Looks Like
  • State explicitly what success looks like by end of call Required
  • "I want us to leave with three named accounts and a committed timeline" Example
  • Partner must agree — vague agreement is not agreement Required
THE FIFTH COMPONENT — PERMISSION TO SAY NO
The most underused part of the UFC: explicitly give the partner permission to say no at any point. "If at any point this stops making sense for you, I want you to tell me — don't just go quiet. I'd rather know now than find out at the end of the quarter." This disarms defensiveness and produces more honest conversations.

UFC Templates by Interaction Type

First Partner Meeting UFC

"Today I want to understand your business well enough to know if we're a real fit — and I'd like you to do the same for us. If it makes sense to go further, we'll agree on next steps before we hang up. If it doesn't, I'd rather know that today. We have 45 minutes. Does that work?"

Co-Sell Deal Kickoff UFC

"I want to walk through this opportunity, agree on our roles, set a timeline to the close, and make sure we're both committed before either of us invests more time. We have 30 minutes. By the end, I want us to have a deal plan or a mutual decision to walk away."

QBR Opening UFC

"The goal today is to look at last quarter's results honestly, agree on where we missed and why, and commit to a specific action plan for next quarter. I'm not here to present slides — I'm here to have a real conversation. We have 90 minutes. Agreed?"

Tough Conversation UFC

"I want to talk about [issue] directly — I think we both need to say some things that might be uncomfortable. I'd rather do this now than let it fester. I'll be straight with you, and I'm asking you to do the same. Twenty minutes."

UFC in Action

What happens when you actually open with a UFC.

Most partner meetings start with pleasantries, then drift into a product update, then end with "let's stay in touch." The UFC changes the architecture of the conversation from the first sentence.

The Partner Who Wants to Wing It

Some partners push back on structure. "Let's just see where the conversation goes." This is usually a sign that they don't want to be held accountable to anything specific. The UFC is exactly what this relationship needs.
HANDLING THE PUSHBACK
"I'm fine with a relaxed conversation — I just want to make sure we both walk away with something useful. Can we at least agree on what useful looks like for each of us by the end?" This is low-resistance and gets the same result as a formal UFC.

Setting Commit Expectations on Timelines

The most valuable UFC in channel sales is the one you set around deal timelines. Before you do any co-sell work on an opportunity, establish with the partner: What is the end-customer's decision timeline? What does the partner commit to doing, and by when? What do you commit to doing, and by when? What happens if either party misses their commitment?

DEAL TIMELINE UFC EXAMPLE
"Before I invest time on this opportunity, I want to agree on the deal structure. You said the customer is deciding by end of Q2. I'll commit to the demo and technical support if you commit to getting me in front of the economic buyer by [date]. If that meeting doesn't happen, we need to reassess whether this is real. Does that work for you?"

Closing Every Meeting with a UFC on Next Steps

End every meeting the same way you started — with a clear agreement. What is each party doing next? By when? When do you talk again? Do not leave a meeting without a confirmed next step that both parties have explicitly agreed to. "I'll follow up" is not a next step. "I'll send you the proposal by Thursday and you'll confirm you can present it to the decision-maker by Friday" is a next step.

From the Field

Why UFCs feel awkward at first and essential after the first month.

The Learning Curve

The first time you open a partner call with a formal UFC, it feels over-structured. The partner may seem surprised. Push through it. After a few repetitions, partners come to expect it — and they start showing up to your calls better prepared than to any other vendor's calls. The structure becomes a signal of professionalism.

What UFCs Expose

DIAGNOSTIC VALUE
A partner who resists every UFC is telling you something important: they don't want to be held accountable to specific outcomes. That is a disqualification signal. Partners who produce are almost always willing to commit to clear agreements. Partners who consistently dodge commitment produce accordingly.

UFCs in Partner Emails

The UFC framework works in writing too. When you send a meeting request, include the purpose, expected duration, your agenda items, and the outcome you're working toward. Ask the partner to confirm or modify. This simple practice eliminates the "what is this call about?" confusion that causes partners to show up unprepared or cancel last-minute.
