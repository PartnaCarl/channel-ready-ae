∨Channel Activation System™ — Up-Front Contract

Every misaligned partner meeting starts the same way — without a UFC.

The Up-Front Contract is Sandler's tool for eliminating ambiguity before a conversation begins. It establishes the agenda, the purpose, the expected outcomes, and the next step — mutually agreed, before the meeting starts. It turns every partner interaction from a status update into a structured conversation with a defined destination.

In direct sales, the UFC manages the buyer-seller dynamic. In channel sales, it manages a more complex web: vendor AE, partner rep, partner leadership, and often end-customer contacts simultaneously. Without a UFC, every participant has a different expectation of what the meeting is for — and when it ends without a clear outcome, everyone blames someone else.

The Core Insight

Partners don't respect your time by default. You teach them to respect it by setting expectations before every conversation — and holding to them. The UFC is how you teach that lesson without making it feel like a lesson.

What You'll Learn

The full UFC structure and how to adapt it for partner onboarding, QBRs, joint sales calls, and co-sell commitment conversations. Why UFCs strengthen partner relationships instead of making them feel transactional.

What You'll Practice

Opening four types of partner conversations with a UFC. Responding when partners try to skip the agenda. Adapting the UFC for asynchronous communication — email and Slack.

What Changes After

Fewer meetings that end with "I'll get back to you." More conversations that produce decisions. Partners begin to prepare for your calls instead of treating them as interruptions.

The four-part UFC structure — and how it maps to channel.

Every UFC has four components: Purpose (why we're talking), Agenda (what we'll cover), Outcome (what success looks like at the end), and Next Step (what happens after we hang up). The UFC is not a monologue — it's a mutual agreement. The partner must confirm each component before the conversation begins.

UFC Script — Partner Onboarding Call

"I want to make sure this call is useful for both of us. My goal is to understand your customer base and current vendor mix well enough to know whether there's a real opportunity for us to work together — and if there is, what the first 90 days should look like. By the end, I'd like us to agree on whether to move forward or whether this isn't the right fit right now. Does that work for you?"

UFC Script — Quarterly Business Review

"Before we get into the numbers, I want to set up what I'm hoping we accomplish today. I want to look at last quarter honestly, understand what drove the results we got, and leave with a specific plan for next quarter that we're both committed to — including named action items and owners. Is that the kind of QBR you're prepared for?"

UFC Script — Joint Sales Call

"Before we jump on with the customer, let's align on our roles. I'll take the lead on the technical questions since you know this customer's environment. You open with the relationship context. By the end, we both want a clear next step agreed with the customer — ideally a POC approval or a date for the decision conversation. Agreed?"

UFC Script — Co-Sell Commitment Conversation

"I want to ask you to commit to something specific on this deal before I invest more time. I'm going to tell you what I need from you, and I need you to tell me honestly whether you can deliver it — or whether this is a deal I should work with a different partner. Fair?"

When partners try to skip the UFC — and what to do about it.

Some partners will push back on the UFC structure — not explicitly, but by trying to jump straight into the content without agreeing to the agenda. They'll open with "so what have you got for me?" or "let's just see what you're working on." This is the moment you hold the UFC.

The Partner Who Rushes Past the Agenda

Response

"I'll absolutely get there — before I do, I want to make sure we're aligned on what we're trying to accomplish today so we don't waste each other's time. Two minutes on setup and then we'll get into it." Then deliver the UFC. Don't skip it because a partner is impatient — that impatience is a personality signal (DISC D-type), not a reason to abandon structure.

The UFC in Asynchronous Communication

The UFC adapts to written communication. Before sending a proposal, a business plan, or a co-sell support package, set the UFC in the covering email: "I'm attaching the analysis you requested. Before you share it with your customer, I need us to agree on a next step — specifically, when you'll schedule the customer presentation and what your preferred close timeline is. Can you confirm that before we move forward?"

The Most Common UFC Mistake

Asking for confirmation at the end of the meeting instead of at the beginning. "Does that make sense?" at the end of a presentation is not a UFC. A UFC is the agreement established before you invest your time — not the confirmation you seek after you've already given it away.

What changes when the UFC becomes your default opening move.

Partners Who Prepare for Calls With You

When partners know you'll open with a UFC and hold them to the agenda, they start preparing for your calls differently. They pull their pipeline data before the QBR instead of during it. They have an answer to "what are you committed to this quarter?" instead of a vague response about trying harder. The UFC trains partners to treat your time as valuable — because you've demonstrated that you do.

The UFC and Deal Velocity

Deals that begin with a UFC close faster. The reason is structural: when both parties agree on the next step before the meeting ends, there's no ambiguity about what happens next. No "I'll think about it" — because the outcome was agreed at the start. The UFC compresses the time between conversations by eliminating the renegotiation of next steps that happens when no UFC was set.
