Why This Module Exists

Every free demo, proposal, and architecture session you give away is a signal that your time has no value.

No-Free-Consulting is not about being difficult. It's about establishing that your expertise is worth something — and that partners who want it need to reciprocate with real commitment before you invest.

The channel AE who says yes to every partner request becomes the partner's research department. Write the proposal. Build the architecture diagram. Run the demo for an unqualified customer. Write the executive summary. All unpaid. All without commitment. And when the deal goes to a different vendor, the AE wonders why they lost.

The Sandler rule is simple: before you invest time in any activity that benefits the partner, get something real in return. Not a promise. Not enthusiasm. A concrete commitment — access to the decision-maker, a confirmed timeline, a registered deal, a meeting on the books. If the partner won't give you that, they're not qualified to receive your investment.

THE CORE INSIGHT
Partners who receive free consulting have no incentive to commit. When you make your involvement contingent on partner commitment, you create a mutual accountability that didn't exist before — and you attract the partners who are actually serious.

What You'll Practice

What Changes After

The NFC Framework

What counts as free consulting — and what to ask for before you give it.

The Four Most Commonly Given Away

1. Demos for Unqualified Opportunities

Running a demo before you've confirmed that the end customer has real pain, a real timeline, and real budget authority. A demo without qualification is a free product education session. The partner gets what they need for the next vendor conversation.

2. Proposals Before Commitment

Writing a formal proposal — pricing, configuration, implementation scope — before the partner has committed to a deal reg and confirmed the customer is the economic buyer. Proposals are expensive to produce. They should only go to qualified, committed opportunities.

3. Architecture and Solution Design

Building out a full solution architecture, integration map, or technical design before the customer has committed to evaluation. This is your intellectual property. Once you've given it to a partner, they can share it with your competitor's rep or use it to negotiate against you.

4. Ongoing Enablement for Non-Producing Partners

Running training sessions, providing updated materials, attending partner events, and answering questions for partners who have not produced a deal in 90+ days. Enablement is an investment — it should be reserved for partners who reciprocate.

The Exchange-Before-Investment Table

WHAT TO ASK FOR BEFORE YOU GIVE
Before a demo: confirmed economic buyer attending, deal registration submitted, timeline stated by the customer in writing.

Before a proposal: pain confirmed at Level 3+, decision-maker identified, timeline confirmed, deal registered.

Before solution architecture: customer has passed a formal technical qualification, formal evaluation kickoff meeting on the books with your pre-sales team.

Before ongoing enablement: partner produced at least one deal in the last 90 days, or has named accounts actively in pipeline.
NFC in Action

How to protect your time without burning the partner relationship.

The fear of saying no to a partner request is the same fear that causes AEs to give everything away. The reality: partners who respect professional standards understand a value exchange. Partners who get angry when you require commitment before investment are telling you they weren't planning to reciprocate anyway.

When a Partner Asks for a Demo First

Most partners default to "can we get a demo?" as a first step. It feels easier than doing qualification work. Your job is to make qualification feel like a natural prerequisite.
RESPONSE TO "CAN WE GET A DEMO?"
"Absolutely — I want to make sure the demo is as relevant as possible for your customer. Help me understand: has the customer confirmed they're evaluating this category? And who from their side will be on the call? I want to make sure we have the right people in the room so I can focus the demo on what matters most to them."

This isn't a no. It's a qualification gate. If the partner can't answer these questions, the demo isn't ready.

When a Partner Asks for a Proposal "Just to Show the Customer a Number"

RESPONSE TO "JUST A ROUGH NUMBER"
"I can get you a ballpark range right now — it typically runs between $X and $Y depending on configuration. For an actual proposal, I need to make sure we're putting in the right configuration. Can you get me 20 minutes with the customer's IT lead before I build it out? I want the proposal to be right, not just close."

Give the range verbally. Protect the formal proposal for qualified opportunities.

How to Say No Without Saying No

The most effective NFC response doesn't refuse — it redirects. Instead of "I can't do that without a deal reg," try "Let's make sure this is worth your customer's time before we bring in my pre-sales team. What do we know about their timeline and budget?" You're not withholding — you're protecting both parties' time from a conversation that isn't ready.

From the Field

The long-term effect of enforcing professional standards with partners.

What Happens to Your Partnership Quality

When you consistently require commitment before investment, you attract a different quality of partner over time. Partners who have operated in a vendor relationship where everything was free will initially push back. Some will go to a competitor who will give them everything free. That's fine — those partners were costing you time without producing revenue. The partners who stay are the ones who were ready to operate like real business partners all along.

The Proposal Win Rate Effect

FIELD OBSERVATION
AEs who implement No-Free-Consulting typically see their proposal count drop by 40-60% in the first 90 days — and their proposal win rate increase by the same margin. They're writing fewer proposals but winning more of them, because they're only writing for real opportunities. The net revenue effect is positive within one quarter.

NFC and Partner Perception

Here's the counterintuitive part: partners who initially push back on your qualification requirements almost always develop more respect for you after you enforce them consistently. They learn that your time and expertise have real value. That perception is what makes them advocate for you internally when a customer asks for a recommendation — because they know you don't give your best work to everyone equally.
