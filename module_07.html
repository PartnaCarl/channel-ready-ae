∨The RELAY Framework™ — No-Free-Consulting

Every hour of free consulting you give away is margin you're paying to a competitor.

The No-Free-Consulting framework is the operational expression of I/R Theory. When a channel AE has separated their identity from their role, they stop needing to prove their value through effort and generosity. That psychological shift makes the No-Free-Consulting conversation possible — because it comes from a place of strength, not deprivation.

The framework defines three categories of channel work: what you do for free (relationship building, general education, qualification conversations), what requires a light commitment (product briefings, solution overviews, initial pain diagnosis), and what requires explicit partner commitment before you engage (solution architecture, custom proposals, proof-of-concept scoping, business plans). The goal is not to become transactional — it's to ensure that the investment of your expertise is matched by the partner's investment of their commitment.

The Core Insight

The partner who gets your architecture, your proposal, and your business plan without committing to anything is the partner who takes all three to your cheapest competitor the next day. The No-Free-Consulting framework prevents this without ending the relationship.

What You'll Practice

Setting the No-Free-Consulting expectation early in a new partner relationship. Running the commitment-before-engagement conversation without making it feel transactional. Responding to a partner who pushes back on the requirement.

What Changes After

Partners who want your expertise earn it. Your time goes to qualified, committed relationships. Your win rate on proposals increases because you only write them for deals that have already been qualified.

The three-tier framework — what's free, what requires light commitment, and what requires explicit commitment.

Tier 1 — Free

Relationship and education

General product briefings, program overview conversations, industry education, relationship-building calls. No deal attached. No specific customer in scope. These are the conversations that build the relationship foundation — and they should be genuinely generous.

Tier 2 — Light Commitment Required

Qualified interest, named account

Detailed solution overview for a named customer, competitive comparison for a specific opportunity, pain diagnosis for a deal that's been registered. Requires deal registration or a named account before the conversation starts.

Tier 3 — Explicit Commitment Required

Architecture, proposals, business plans

Solution architecture, custom proposal writing, POC scoping, partner business plan development, pricing analysis. Requires an explicit written commitment from the partner before any work begins — what they're committing to in terms of timeline, exclusivity, and next step if the work produces what they need.

The Commitment-Before-Engagement Conversation

Setting the Expectation Early in a New Partner Relationship

"I want to be a great resource for you and your customers. The way I work is: for general questions and education, I'm always available. For anything that involves specific customer deals — architecture, proposals, business plans — I'll need you to have a registered opportunity or a named account before I invest time building something custom. That's not about being difficult — it's about making sure the work I do for you actually closes deals rather than sitting in a folder. Does that work for you?"

After a partner has taken your work to a competitor — what to do next.

It happens. You've done the work — written the proposal, built the architecture, developed the business plan — and the partner has taken it to a cheaper vendor. How you respond to this moment determines whether it happens again.

The IP Protection Conversation After the Fact

"I heard you went with [competitor] on the [customer] deal. I want to be direct with you about something: the proposal and architecture we built together took significant time and expertise, and I'd invested it based on an understanding that we were working this deal together. I'm not going to pretend that didn't affect my trust in how we work together. Going forward, I'm going to need explicit commitment before I build anything custom — not because I don't want to work with you, but because I need to know that what I build is going toward a real opportunity. Can we reset on that basis?"

When the Partner Pushes Back on No-Free-Consulting

NRS Applied to the Pushback

"I hear you — and I understand that's not how you've worked with other vendors. I want to make sure we're right for each other before we go further. Most vendors will build you proposals without commitment because they're fighting for your attention. I'm not doing that — not because I don't value the relationship, but because the work I do is worth the commitment I'm asking for. If that's not the right model for how you work, I completely understand."

No-Free-Consulting in practice — what it protects beyond IP.

It Protects the Partner Relationship Too

Counter-intuitively, the No-Free-Consulting framework protects the partner relationship as much as it protects your time. Partners who get custom work without committing to anything don't value the work — because they haven't invested anything in receiving it. Partners who've had to commit something before receiving your expertise treat the output differently. They've earned it, and that ownership changes how they sell with it.

The Long-Term Effect on Partner Behavior

After 6–12 months of consistent No-Free-Consulting, the partners in your portfolio change their behavior in a predictable way: the partners who were never going to commit stop asking for custom work, and the partners who are genuinely motivated become more efficient in how they request your support. Your time allocation shifts toward the relationships that produce revenue — without you having to explicitly manage anyone out.
