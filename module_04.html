The Partner Fit Filter doesn't change in channel. What changes is who has the pain.

In direct sales, the Partner Fit Filter uncovers the buyer's pain. In channel sales, there are two pain sources to diagnose: the partner's pain (why they're not selling as much as they should be) and the end-customer's pain (why the customer needs your product). A channel AE who confuses these two types of pain — or who skips pain diagnosis entirely and jumps to a product pitch — produces conversations that partners forget before the call ends.

The Partner Fit Filter's three levels move from surface-level problem to business impact to real pain. The goal is not to find out what the partner or customer likes about your product. The goal is to find out what it costs them to not have it — in lost revenue, customer churn, operational friction, or competitive disadvantage. That cost is the foundation of every compelling argument for moving forward.

The Core Insight

Partners don't buy on features. They sell on customer pain. Your job is to help them articulate that pain before they're in front of the customer — so they're not figuring it out in real time at your expense.

What You'll Learn

The three levels of pain in both partner qualification and joint customer conversations. How to apply Partner Fit Filter questions in a partner-first, customer-second sequence that prepares the partner to sell.

What You'll Practice

Diagnosing partner pain in the onboarding and QBR context. Running a joint customer call where the Partner Fit Filter drives the discovery instead of a feature walkthrough. Recognizing when a partner is describing feature interest, not real pain.

What Changes After

Partners come to joint calls prepared to talk about customer pain instead of product specs. Your win rate on co-sell deals improves because discovery happens before the demo, not during it.

Two Partner Fit Filters: Partner pain and customer pain.

Diagnosing Partner Pain

Partner pain is the friction the partner experiences in their current vendor mix — missed revenue, customer escalations, margin compression, or competitive pressure. It's the reason they'd allocate more time and energy to selling your product over alternatives. If you can't articulate the partner's pain in their own terms, you haven't done the qualification work.

Partner Fit Filter — Level 1

"How are your current vendor relationships working for you in this space?" (Surface problem)

Partner Fit Filter — Level 2

"What does that cost you in terms of deals you've lost or margin you've had to give up?" (Business impact)

Partner Fit Filter — Level 3

"If this keeps up for another year, what does that mean for your business?" (Real pain — the future cost of inaction)

Diagnosing Customer Pain in a Joint Call

On joint customer calls, the Partner Fit Filter runs through the partner — not around them. The partner has the customer relationship; you have the diagnostic framework. Prepare the partner to ask the Partner Fit Filter questions themselves, with your coaching in advance and your support in the room.

Customer Partner Fit Filter — Joint Call Setup (Coach the Partner Before)

"In this call, let's lead with their environment before we talk about our product. Ask them: what's the current state of [problem area], what's it costing them, and how long it's been an issue. That diagnostic is worth more than any demo we could run."

Feature interest vs. real pain — the most important distinction in channel.

The most common failure mode in channel co-sell calls: the partner opens with a product pitch instead of a pain diagnosis, the customer asks a few feature questions, the demo runs without any pain established, and the deal stalls at evaluation with no urgency to close.

Feature interest is "that looks useful." Real pain is "we've lost three customers this year because we couldn't solve this." The closing velocity on a real-pain deal is 3–5x faster than on a feature-interest deal because the customer has a compelling reason to act. Your job in the co-sell motion is to help the partner run the conversation that surfaces real pain — not the conversation that produces a lukewarm demo request.

What Partners Say vs. What They Mean

Partner Says

"My customers love this product."

What they mean: a few customers have responded positively to the demo. No Partner Fit Filter has been run. No urgency has been established. This is feature interest dressed up as opportunity.

Partner Says

"I have a hot deal for you."

What they mean: a customer is interested. Pain may or may not have been diagnosed. Ask: "What pain is driving the urgency? What happens if they don't solve this in the next 90 days?" If they can't answer, it's not as hot as they think.

Redirecting a Feature Pitch Back to Pain

"Before we walk through the product, help me understand your customer's current situation. What problem are they trying to solve, and what's it costing them today? That's going to make the demo a lot more relevant."

Field Notes: The Partner Fit Filter in the wild.

The Partner Who Thinks They Know the Pain

Experienced partners often short-circuit the Partner Fit Filter because they believe they already know what their customer needs. Resist the urge to let them. Even accurate assumptions miss nuance that the Partner Fit Filter surfaces — and that nuance is often the difference between a stalled evaluation and a closed deal. Run the Partner Fit Filter even when you think you know the answer.

The Level 3 Pain Shortcut

Level 3 pain — the real, emotionally charged pain — often comes out in casual conversation rather than structured discovery. Train yourself to listen for it everywhere: a partner's offhand comment about a customer who threatened to switch vendors, a customer's sigh when you mention a competitor's name, a technical contact's frustration with a process that "we've been trying to fix for two years." These are Level 3 signals. Stop and go deeper.

Using Partner Fit Filter Findings in QBRs

Document the pain you've uncovered in every co-sell engagement and bring it to the QBR. "Last quarter we found three customers in your base with [specific pain]. We helped you close two of them. This quarter, here are four more customers with the same pain signal that we haven't engaged yet." Partner Fit Filter findings become a pipeline generation tool when systematized across the partnership.

The Partner Segmentation Model — Qualification Differs by Type

This segmentation is drawn from cybersecurity channel experience (Natalie — Expel, Mimecast, Okta, Varonis) but the partner-type logic transfers to any B2B technology company with a VAR, MSP, or SI ecosystem.

Partner qualification is not one-size-fits-all. Different partner types have different buying motions, different decision criteria, and different reasons for engaging with your product. The Partner Fit Filter must be calibrated to the partner type you're qualifying.

Type 1

Big Box / Transactional

CDW, SHI, Zones, Connection. High volume, low touch. They want SKU availability, competitive pricing, and deal registration speed. Qualification focus: do they have active pipeline in your category, or are you just another line card?

Type 2

Security Advisory / Services-Led

Guidepoint, Optiv, Presidio. They sell consulting hours and wrap your product into an engagement. Qualification focus: do they have a practice area that maps to your solution, and will they co-invest in enablement?

Type 3

M365 Attach / Licensing

SoftwareOne, Insight. They lead with Microsoft licensing and attach security or infrastructure products. Qualification focus: is your product positioned as an add-on to their existing motion, or are you asking them to build a new one?

Type 4

Regional / Emerging

Smaller, hungrier, more willing to invest time. But they lack the infrastructure to scale. Qualification focus: can they actually close the deal types you need, or will you end up doing the selling for them?

Qualification Principle

The same Partner Fit Filter questions apply to every partner type — but the answers you're listening for are completely different. A Big Box partner who says "we need more enablement" means something different than a Regional partner saying the same thing.

Do This Monday

Look at your top three active co-sell deals. For each one, ask: have I diagnosed the customer's Level 3 pain — the cost of inaction — or am I assuming it from their stated interest? For any deal where you can't answer that with a specific dollar amount or business consequence, go back to the partner this week and run the Partner Fit Filter at Level 3 before the deal progresses further.
